Two Numbers You Need to Know

The Legal Minimum

ESA — What They Must Pay

Every province has an Employment Standards Act that sets a minimum termination pay based on years of service. This is the number on your termination letter.

In Ontario, the maximum is 8 weeks. In most provinces, it caps around 8–16 weeks.

What You Can Negotiate

Common Law — What Courts Award

Under common law (based on real court cases), courts award reasonable notice that often runs 12–24 months, far more than the ESA floor.

This is calculated using the Bardal factors: your age, position, years of service, and ability to find new work.

Common Questions

What is the ESA minimum?

The Employment Standards Act sets the legal floor for termination pay in each province. Your employer must pay at least this — but common law typically entitles you to much more.

What is common law entitlement?

Canadian courts use the Bardal factors (age, service, position, job availability) to set "reasonable notice." This is what you can negotiate or sue for — often 5 to 15 times the ESA minimum.

Should I sign immediately?

No. You almost always have time to consult a lawyer first. Many employment lawyers offer free consultations and work on contingency — no upfront cost.

What are Bardal Factors?

From the 1960 landmark case Bardal v. Globe & Mail: courts weigh your age, years of service, position level, and availability of similar work to determine reasonable notice.

Is this calculator accurate?

It provides educational estimates based on current provincial legislation and Canadian case law. Every case is unique — this tool helps you understand your starting point before speaking with a lawyer.

What is wrongful dismissal?

Wrongful dismissal means your employer terminated you without providing reasonable notice or pay in lieu. It does not require illegal or immoral behaviour — simply ending employment without proper compensation qualifies.
